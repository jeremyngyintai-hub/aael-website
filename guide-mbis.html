強制驗樓計劃（MBIS）：收到通知後的完整流程Mandatory Building Inspection Scheme: What Happens After the Notice

躍昇建築事務顧問有限公司　·　更新於 2026年7月Ascend Architecture & Engineering Ltd　·　Updated July 2026

強制驗樓的法定通知不是罰單，而是一份有時限的工作清單。理解流程之後，多數業主會發現：真正的壓力來自時間安排，而不是技術本身。An MBIS statutory notice is not a penalty — it is a work list with a deadline. Once the process is clear, most owners find the pressure is about scheduling rather than technical difficulty.

收到的是甚麼

強制驗樓計劃（MBIS）針對樓齡達到指定年期的私人樓宇。被抽選的樓宇會收到法定通知，要求業主委任註冊檢驗人員，就樓宇的公用部分、外牆及伸出物進行「訂明檢驗」，並在需要時進行「訂明修葺」。

強制驗窗計劃（MWIS）性質相近，但針對窗戶，由合資格人士進行檢驗及修葺。兩者可能同時發出。

兩個階段：檢驗與修葺

  1. 訂明檢驗——註冊檢驗人員檢查樓宇狀況，判斷是否需要修葺，並向屋宇署提交檢驗報告。若檢驗結果顯示無須修葺，程序到此完成。
  2. 訂明修葺——若檢驗發現須修葺的項目，業主須委任註冊承建商施工，並由註冊檢驗人員監督，完成後提交完工報告。
常見誤解

不少業主以為收到通知即等於樓宇有危險、一定要大修。實際上檢驗的目的正是判斷需要做甚麼；部分樓宇檢驗後只需小量修葺，甚至無須修葺。先做檢驗，才知範圍——在檢驗前預設最壞情況並無意義。

時限與延期

法定通知會列明遵辦期限。若因合理原因無法在期限內完成（例如工程規模較大、法團需時召開業主大會通過撥款），可向屋宇署申請延期，但必須有實際進度支持，而非單純拖延。

不遵辦通知屬刑事罪行，可被檢控及罰款；署方亦可代為安排檢驗或修葺並向業主追討費用。

業主立案法團的額外考慮

樓宇公用部分的檢驗及修葺涉及全體業主，實務上須處理：

  • 召開業主大會、通過決議及撥款
  • 按《建築物管理條例》規定的採購程序招標
  • 工程期間的溝通、投訴處理及進度監察
  • 公帑資助計劃（如適用）的申請程序

這些行政程序往往比工程本身耗時。收到通知後及早展開，可避免在期限前才發現流程走不完。

選擇檢驗人員時應留意

註冊檢驗人員必須列於屋宇署名冊。除資格之外，值得考慮的是：該公司能否在檢驗發現問題後，一併處理後續的設計、入則及監督。如果檢驗與修葺分屬不同顧問，業主須額外承擔協調成本；由同一團隊跟進，由檢驗、報告到完工證明可一線到底。

本文為一般說明，實際要求以屋宇署發出的通知及最新指引為準。

What the notice is

The Mandatory Building Inspection Scheme (MBIS) applies to private buildings of a specified age. Selected buildings receive a statutory notice requiring the owner to appoint a Registered Inspector to carry out a prescribed inspection of common parts, external walls and projections, and prescribed repair where needed.

The Mandatory Window Inspection Scheme (MWIS) works similarly for windows, using qualified persons. Both may arrive together.

Two stages: inspection, then repair

  1. Prescribed inspection — the Registered Inspector examines the building, determines whether repair is required and files the inspection report with the Buildings Department. If no repair is needed, the process ends there.
  2. Prescribed repair — where repairs are identified, a registered contractor carries them out under the Registered Inspector's supervision, and a completion report follows.
A common misconception

Many owners assume a notice means the building is dangerous and major works are inevitable. The inspection exists precisely to determine what is needed; some buildings require only minor repair, or none. Assuming the worst before inspection helps no one.

Deadlines and extensions

The notice states a compliance period. Where completion is genuinely not possible in time — larger works, or an owners' corporation needing a general meeting to approve funding — an extension may be sought from the Department, supported by real progress rather than simple delay.

Non-compliance is an offence and may be prosecuted; the Department may also arrange the inspection or repair and recover costs from owners.

Extra considerations for owners' corporations

  • Convening general meetings, passing resolutions and approving funds
  • Procurement and tendering under the Building Management Ordinance
  • Communication, complaint handling and progress monitoring during works
  • Applying for subsidy schemes where eligible

These administrative steps often take longer than the works. Starting early avoids discovering near the deadline that the process cannot be completed in time.

Choosing an inspector

A Registered Inspector must be on the Buildings Department register. Beyond qualification, consider whether the practice can also carry the follow-on design, submission and supervision. Where inspection and repair sit with different consultants, the owner absorbs the coordination cost; handled by one team, the line runs unbroken from inspection to completion certification.

General guidance only; the operative requirements are those in the notice and the Buildings Department's current guidance.
