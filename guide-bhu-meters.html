簡樸房獨立水錶電錶：要求、申請程序與常見延誤Separate Water and Electricity Meters for Basic Housing Units

躍昇建築事務顧問有限公司　·　更新於 2026年7月Ascend Architecture & Engineering Ltd　·　Updated July 2026

在所有最低標準之中，獨立水電錶是最容易被低估、卻最常拖慢整個認證進度的一項。原因很簡單：這一項不是工程問題，是申請程序問題。Of all the minimum standards, separate metering is the most underestimated and the most likely to delay certification — because it is not a construction problem, it is an application problem.

條例要求的究竟是甚麼

簡樸房標準要求每個分間單位設有獨立的水錶及電錶，而關鍵在於「由誰提供」：

  • 水錶——須為水務署提供的獨立水錶
  • 電錶——須為電力公司（中電或港燈，視乎所在區域）提供的獨立電錶
內部分錶為何不合規

不少舊有分間單位由電工在單位內加裝分錶，按錶收費。這種安排在條例下並不符合要求，因為計量並非由公用事業機構提供，租戶亦沒有與供應商的直接帳戶關係。改造時必須正式申請。

申請的實際流程

整體而言分三步：

  1. 技術可行性評估——電力方面須確認總負荷、電錶房位置及線路走向是否容納得到新增錶位；食水方面須確認喉管改動的可行性。舊樓的電錶房空間往往是最大限制。
  2. 正式申請——向電力公司及水務署遞交申請，附所需圖則及文件。涉及公用部分的改動時，可能需要業主立案法團或大廈管理方同意。
  3. 工程配合及裝錶——內部線路及喉管改動由持牌承辦商施工，通過檢查後由供應商安裝錶具。

時間，是這一項的真正成本

相比拆牆鋪磚，申請程序無法靠加人手加快。由評估、申請到實際裝錶，需時可以由數星期至數月不等，視乎樓宇狀況、供電容量及是否涉及公用部分。

這正是我們反覆強調及早開始的原因：改造工程可以壓縮，行政流程壓縮不了。若把水電錶申請留到寬限期後段才處理，很容易變成整個認證計劃的樽頸。

常見延誤原因

情況影響
電錶房空間不足須重新設計錶位或改動公用設施，需額外協調及審批
總供電負荷不足可能須申請增加供電容量，時間顯著延長
涉及大廈公用部分須取得法團或管理公司同意，視乎會議周期
喉管改動觸及結構須由註冊結構工程師評估，甚至需要入則
樓宇尚有未遵辦命令可能同時阻礙認證申請，須一併處理

與其他工程一併規劃

實務上最有效率的做法，是在首次評估時就把水電錶、通風、防火及結構一併看清楚，做出一份完整的改造方案，而不是逐項發現、逐項處理。分開處理的話，往往出現「牆已砌好，才發現錶位要改，又要重新開牆」的情況。

由於本公司的認可人士、註冊結構工程師及註冊專業測量師同屬一司，評估、方案、入則以至核證可在同一個工作計劃中完成，減少業主在不同顧問之間來回等候的時間。

本文為一般說明，實際申請要求以水務署、電力公司及房屋局最新公布為準。

What the Ordinance actually requires

Each subdivided unit needs its own water and electricity meter — and the critical point is who provides them:

  • Water — an individual meter provided by the Water Supplies Department
  • Electricity — an individual meter provided by the power company (CLP or HK Electric, depending on area)
Why internal sub-meters do not qualify

Many existing subdivided flats use sub-meters fitted by an electrician, with the landlord billing by reading. That does not satisfy the requirement: the metering is not provided by the utility and the tenant has no direct account with the supplier. A formal application is needed.

The process in practice

  1. Technical feasibility — confirming total load, meter room capacity and cable routing for electricity; pipework feasibility for water. In older buildings, meter room space is usually the binding constraint.
  2. Formal application — submissions to the utilities with the required drawings and documents. Where common parts are affected, owners' corporation or management consent may be needed.
  3. Works and installation — internal wiring and pipework by licensed contractors, then meter installation by the supplier after inspection.

Time is the real cost here

Unlike construction, application lead times cannot be shortened by adding labour. From assessment to meters in place can run from several weeks to several months depending on the building, supply capacity and whether common parts are involved.

This is exactly why starting early matters: works can be compressed, administration cannot. Left to the back end of the grace period, metering becomes the bottleneck for the whole certification.

Common causes of delay

SituationEffect
Insufficient meter room spaceRedesign or alteration of common facilities; extra coordination and approval
Inadequate supply capacityMay require a supply upgrade application, adding significant time
Common parts affectedOwners' corporation or management consent needed, subject to meeting cycles
Pipework touching structureStructural engineer assessment, possibly a statutory submission
Outstanding orders on the buildingMay block certification in parallel; must be dealt with together

Plan it with everything else

The efficient approach is to look at metering, ventilation, fire safety and structure together at the first assessment and produce one rectification scheme — rather than discovering issues one at a time. Piecemeal work produces the classic outcome: walls built, then opened again because the meter position has to move.

With our Authorized Person, Registered Structural Engineer and Registered Professional Surveyor (R.P.S.) in the same practice, assessment, scheme, submission and certification run in a single programme instead of a queue between consultants.

General guidance only; requirements are as published by the Water Supplies Department, the power companies and the Housing Bureau.
